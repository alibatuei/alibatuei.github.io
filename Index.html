A minimalist guide to key English grammar points, with examples from academic medical texts.

Guide based on "Medical Literacy" and "English Grammar in Use".

Part 1: Present & Past Tenses

1.1 Present Continuous

+
  • Formula: be + -ing (I am doing)
  • Use: For actions happening now or over a limited, temporary period.
  • Suggestion: Think of this as the "temporary" tense. It's for what's in progress.
  • Example: Researchers and doctors are constantly pushing the boundaries of what was thought impossible.
  • Watch Out!: Avoid using this for state verbs like `know` or `like`. Say "He knows," not "He is knowing."
  • In Context: Groundbreaking treatments and therapies are being developed to tackle previously incurable diseases. Clinical trials and experiments are being conducted to assess the effectiveness of these innovative approaches.
  • Test: What's all that noise? What _______ (happen)?

1.2 Present Simple

+
  • Formula: verb (I do)
  • Use: For habits, repeated actions, and general truths or facts.
  • Suggestion: Think of this as the "permanent" or "fact" tense. Signal words: usually, always, every day.
  • Example: The muscular system is one of the most important systems in the human body.
  • Watch Out!: Always remember the `-s` for he/she/it (e.g., "The heart pump**s** blood...").
  • In Context: The skin is the largest organ in the human body, and it is made up of three layers. The dermis is the middle layer and contains nerve endings, hair follicles, and sweat glands. Hormones act as chemical messengers.
  • Test: Bones _______ (be) the primary component of the skeletal system.

1.3 Past Simple

+
  • Formula: verb-ed / irregular form (I did)
  • Use: For a completed action at a specific, finished time in the past.
  • Suggestion: If you can ask "When did it happen?" and get a specific past answer (e.g., *yesterday*, *last week*), use the Past Simple.
  • Example: A 45-year-old male patient presented with a history of persistent pain.
  • Watch Out!: In questions and negatives, use `did` with the base verb, not the past form. (e.g., "**Did** you **report**?" not "Did you reported?").
  • In Context: She reported that the rash began suddenly. On examination, Mary was noted to have a red, raised rash. The physician performed a blood test to check for any potential underlying causes of her symptoms.
  • Test: The blood test _______ (reveal) elevated creatine kinase levels.

1.4 Past Continuous

+
  • Formula: was/were + -ing (I was doing)
  • Use: An action in progress in the past, often interrupted by another action.
  • Suggestion: Use this to "set the scene." It's the "long action" interrupted by a "short action" (Past Simple).
  • Example: The patient was found wandering around the middle of the road.
  • Watch Out!: Some verbs like `know` or `want` are not used in the continuous.
  • In Context: The burning sensation during urination was causing her discomfort, and the lower abdominal pain **had been persistent**. The hospital, which **was undergoing** renovations, had outdated equipment.
  • Test: What _______ you _______ (do) at 10 o'clock last night?

Part 2: Present Perfect & Past

2.1 Present Perfect Simple

+
  • Formula: have/has + past participle (I have done)
  • Use: For a past action that has a result or relevance now.
  • Suggestion: Think of it as a bridge connecting the past to the present. Signal words: just, already, yet, ever.
  • Example: More than 450 conditions have now been described.
  • Watch Out!: Don't use this with finished time words. Say "The rash **began** several days ago," not "The rash has begun several days ago."
  • In Context: In recent years, tremendous progress has been made in the field of creating artificial limbs. Researchers have found that creating a seamless connection is crucial. Very few chemicals have been tested.
  • Test: Over 50 hormones _______ (be identified) in humans.

2.2 Present Perfect Continuous

+
  • Formula: have/has + been + -ing (I have been doing)
  • Use: To focus on the duration of an activity that is still continuing or has just stopped.
  • Suggestion: Ask "How long?" If the duration is the key information, this is often the right tense.
  • Example: To improve my writing skills, I have been attending writing workshops.
  • Watch Out!: To say *how many* or *how much* (the result), use the simple form. ("He **has made** progress," not "He has been making progress").
  • In Context: She mentioned that the symptoms began about two weeks ago and have been gradually worsening. The patient... **has been managing** her conditions with medication and regular check-ups.
  • Test: The patient _______ (experience) a constant urge to urinate.

2.3 Past Perfect

+
  • Formula: had + past participle (I had done)
  • Use: An action that happened *before* another past action.
  • Suggestion: Think of it as the "past of the past." It clarifies the sequence of events.
  • Example: Her medical history revealed... she had experienced multiple episodes of tonsillitis as a child.
  • Watch Out!: Don't overuse it. If actions happen in chronological order, use the Past Simple for all of them.
  • In Context: The nurse, who I had spoken to earlier, administered the wrong medication.
  • Test: He _______ no previous history of any medical conditions.

2.4 Used to

+
  • Formula: used to + verb
  • Use: A past habit or state that is no longer true.
  • Suggestion: This structure is for past habits and is not found in the formal medical text provided.
  • Example (from English Grammar in Use): I used to play tennis a lot, but I don't play very much now.
  • Watch Out!: Don't confuse `I used to do` (past habit) with `I am used to doing` (I am accustomed to it).

  • Test: This town _______ (have) a cinema, but it closed down years ago.

Part 3: The Future

3.1 Future with 'Will'

+
  • Formula: will + verb
  • Use: For future facts, predictions, and results.
  • Suggestion: This is the most common way to talk about the future in formal, factual writing.
  • Example: The hormone-receptor complex will switch on or switch off specific biological processes.
  • Watch Out!: Don't use `will` for fixed plans and arrangements. Say "I'm meeting the doctor tomorrow," not "I will meet...".
  • In Context: The work **is expected** to last two weeks. Next Sunday the road **will be closed**, and traffic **will be diverted**.
  • Test: By the end of the book, you _______ (have) a strong foundation in medical terminology.

Part 4: Modals

4.1 Modals of Possibility & Ability (`can/could/may/might`)

+
  • Formula: modal + verb
  • Use: To express ability, possibility, or potential.
  • Suggestion: In formal texts, `can` and `may` are very common for expressing what is possible under certain conditions.
  • Example: Chemicals **can** interfere with the endocrine system.
  • Watch Out!: `Could` can be the past of `can` ("She **couldn't** go") OR a present possibility ("It **could** be true"). Context is key.
  • In Context: This **can** greatly improve patient comfort. This **could** be used to treat genetic diseases. The condition **may** result from inherited mutations. The symptoms **may** include bone pain.
  • Test: Warts _______ appear anywhere on the skin.

4.2 Modals of Obligation & Advice (`should/must`)

+
  • Formula: should / must + verb
  • Use: To give advice, state a recommendation, or express necessity.
  • Suggestion: `Should` is common for recommendations in medical discussions.
  • Example: You **should** exercise, eat right and see your healthcare provider regularly.
  • In Context: A plural subject **should be** matched with a singular verb. Fractured bones **should be** treated as soon as possible.
  • Test: To use coordinating conjunctions correctly, it _______ (be) important to make sure that each clause has a subject and a verb.

Part 5: If & Wish

5.1 Real Conditionals

+
  • Formula: If + Present -> Present / Modal
  • Use: To talk about real and possible situations, causes, and effects.
  • Suggestion: Medical texts use this constantly to explain conditions. The `if` part states the condition, the other part states the result.
  • Example: **If** the coronary arteries become narrow..., the blood supply **will be** restricted.
  • Watch Out!: The most common mistake is putting `will` in the `if` part.
  • In Context: Melanoma... **can** be deadly **if** left untreated. **If** untreated, rheumatoid arthritis gradually **causes** permanent joint damage. Surgery was also suggested as an option **if** the pain persisted.
  • Test: **If** these risk factor trends continue, CVD mortality rates _______ (increase) in the coming years.

5.2 Unreal Conditionals & Wish

+
  • Formula: If + Past -> would; I wish + Past.
  • Use: For hypothetical situations or to express regrets.
  • Suggestion: This structure is for imagined, unreal situations and is not found in the factual medical text provided.
  • Example (from English Grammar in Use): **If I had** more money, **I would travel** more often. **I wish I lived** closer to my family.
  • In Context: (No examples in this medical text).
  • Test: If the patient _______ (have) a different history, the diagnosis _______ be different. (would / could)
